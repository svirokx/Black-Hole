Known Black Holes

A comprehensive catalog of confirmed and candidate black holes across the universe, organized by class. All data sourced from NASA, ESA, EHT, LIGO/Virgo, and peer-reviewed astrophysical journals.

Supermassive Black Holes

10⁶ – 10¹¹ solar masses. Found at the centers of most large galaxies. They power quasars, AGN, and shape galaxy evolution.

M87* First Image

Mass: 6.5 × 10⁹ M☉
Distance: 55 Mly
Galaxy: Messier 87
Type: Active AGN

First black hole ever directly imaged (April 10, 2019) by the Event Horizon Telescope. A supermassive giant at the heart of the elliptical galaxy Virgo A, powering a relativistic jet extending 5,000 light-years. Its shadow diameter is ~42 micro-arcseconds.

EHT Collaboration, ApJL, 2019

Sagittarius A* Our Galaxy

Mass: 4.15 × 10⁶ M☉
Distance: 26,000 ly
Galaxy: Milky Way
Type: Quiescent SMBH

The supermassive black hole at the center of our Milky Way. Its mass was determined by tracking orbiting stars (S2/S0-2), earning Andrea Ghez and Reinhard Genzel the 2020 Nobel Prize. EHT imaged it in May 2022. Despite its mass, it is remarkably quiet — accreting at a tiny fraction of its Eddington limit.

EHT Collaboration, 2022; Genzel, Ghez et al.
~100 billion M☉

Phoenix A Most Massive Known

Mass: ~1 × 10¹¹ M☉
Distance: 5.7 Bly
Galaxy: Phoenix Cluster BCG
Type: Ultramassive

The most massive black hole discovered to date (announced 2022). Located at the center of the brightest cluster galaxy in the Phoenix Cluster, it has approximately 100 billion solar masses. Its event horizon would engulf our entire solar system many times over.

Hlavacek-Larrondo et al., ApJ, 2022
66 billion M☉

TON 618

Mass: 6.6 × 10¹⁰ M☉
Distance: 10.4 Bly
Type: Hyperluminous quasar
Schwarzschild radius: ~1,300 AU

A hyperluminous quasar powered by one of the most massive black holes known. Its event horizon radius is ~1,300 AU — far beyond Pluto's orbit. The quasar shines with a luminosity of 1.4 × 10⁴¹ W, making it one of the most luminous objects in the universe.

Shemmer et al., ApJ, 2004
40 billion M☉

Holm 15A*

Mass: 4 × 10¹⁰ M☉
Distance: ~700 Mly
Galaxy: Holm 15A (Abell 85)
Type: Ultramassive

Located in the central galaxy of galaxy cluster Abell 85. Its mass was determined through stellar orbit modeling. The galaxy itself has an unusually large, depleted core — likely carved out by the merger of two previous supermassive black holes.

Mehrgan et al., ApJ, 2019
40 billion M☉

S5 0014+81

Mass: 4 × 10¹⁰ M☉
Distance: 12.07 Bly
Type: Blazar / Quasar
Luminosity: 10⁴¹ W

One of the most massive and luminous quasars known, classified as a blazar (jet pointed toward Earth). Its extreme brightness makes it visible across most of the observable universe. The accretion disk alone outshines many entire galaxies.

Ghisellini et al., MNRAS, 2010
21 billion M☉

NGC 4889

Mass: 2.1 × 10¹⁰ M☉
Distance: 336 Mly
Galaxy: NGC 4889 (Coma Cluster)
Type: Quiescent SMBH

One of the most massive black holes in the nearby universe, sitting at the center of the giant elliptical galaxy NGC 4889 in the Coma Cluster. Despite its enormous mass, it is currently dormant — not actively accreting matter. Its event horizon is roughly the size of our solar system.

McConnell et al., Nature, 2011
18 billion M☉

OJ 287 Binary System

Primary mass: 1.8 × 10¹⁰ M☉
Secondary mass: ~1.5 × 10⁸ M☉
Distance: 3.5 Bly
Type: BL Lac / binary SMBH

One of the best-studied binary supermassive black hole systems. The smaller black hole orbits the larger one with a ~12-year period, plunging through the primary's accretion disk twice per orbit and producing bright optical outbursts. This system provides one of the strongest tests of general relativity in the strong-field regime.

Valtonen et al., Nature, 2008
17 billion M☉

NGC 1277

Mass: 1.7 × 10¹⁰ M☉
Distance: 220 Mly
Galaxy: NGC 1277 (Perseus)
BH/Galaxy mass: ~14%

A compact lenticular galaxy in the Perseus Cluster containing an unusually massive black hole — about 14% of the galaxy's total mass (typical is <1%). This extreme ratio challenges our understanding of how black holes and galaxies co-evolve.

van den Bosch et al., Nature, 2012
17 billion M☉

NGC 1600

Mass: 1.7 × 10¹⁰ M☉
Distance: ~200 Mly
Galaxy: NGC 1600
Type: Quiescent

An elliptical galaxy hosting a surprisingly massive black hole for its environment — it resides in a relatively sparse galaxy group rather than a dense cluster. This suggests that ultra-massive black holes can form even outside the densest cosmic environments.

Thomas et al., Nature, 2016
12 billion M☉

SDSS J0100+2802

Mass: 1.2 × 10¹⁰ M☉
Distance: 12.8 Bly (z ≈ 6.3)
Type: High-redshift quasar
Age of universe at emission: ~900 Myr

One of the most massive black holes at very high redshift. It existed when the universe was less than 1 billion years old, posing serious challenges to models of early black hole growth — it's unclear how such a massive object could form so quickly after the Big Bang.

Wu et al., Nature, 2015
9.7 billion M☉

NGC 3842

Mass: 9.7 × 10⁹ M☉
Distance: ~331 Mly
Galaxy: NGC 3842 (Leo Cluster)
Type: BCG SMBH

The central galaxy of the Leo Cluster harboring a near-10-billion-solar-mass black hole. It was among the first "ultra-massive" black holes discovered through stellar dynamics, expanding the known upper mass limit.

McConnell et al., Nature, 2011
886 million M☉

3C 273 First Quasar

Mass: 8.86 × 10⁸ M☉
Distance: 2.4 Bly
Type: Quasar
Discovery: 1963

The first quasar ever identified (by Maarten Schmidt in 1963) and one of the closest. Despite being 2.4 billion light-years away, it is bright enough to be seen in amateur telescopes. It demonstrated that immensely luminous objects exist at cosmological distances, powered by supermassive black holes.

Schmidt, Nature, 1963; Peterson et al., ApJ, 2004
800 million M☉

ULAS J1342+0928

Mass: 8 × 10⁸ M☉
Redshift: z = 7.54
Type: High-z quasar
Age of universe: ~690 Myr

One of the most distant quasars known. It existed when the universe was only 690 million years old — during the epoch of reionization when the first stars and galaxies were forming. Its existence at such an early time is a major puzzle for black hole formation theories.

Bañados et al., Nature, 2018
2.5 billion M☉

Cygnus A

Mass: 2.5 × 10⁹ M☉
Distance: ~760 Mly
Galaxy: Cygnus A
Type: Radio galaxy AGN

One of the strongest radio sources in the sky. Its supermassive black hole powers immense relativistic jets that create spectacular radio lobes extending ~300,000 light-years on each side. It was one of the first extragalactic radio sources discovered (1939).

Tadhunter et al.; Carilli & Barthel, 1996
140 million M☉

Andromeda (M31*)

Mass: 1.4 × 10⁸ M☉
Distance: 2.5 Mly
Galaxy: Andromeda (M31)
Type: Quiescent SMBH

The supermassive black hole in the center of the Andromeda galaxy — the nearest major galaxy to the Milky Way. It is about 35 times more massive than Sgr A*. When the Milky Way and Andromeda merge in ~4.5 billion years, these two SMBHs will eventually spiral together and merge.

Bender et al., ApJ, 2005
55 million M☉

Centaurus A (NGC 5128)

Mass: 5.5 × 10⁷ M☉
Distance: 13 Mly
Galaxy: NGC 5128
Type: Active radio galaxy

The nearest active galaxy and one of the closest radio galaxies. Its distinctive dark dust lane (visible in optical images) is the result of a past galaxy merger. Its jets extend over a million light-years. In 2021, the EHT resolved the jet launching region near the black hole.

Cappellari et al., 2009; EHT, Nature Astronomy, 2021
~40 million M☉

UHZ1 Earliest Known

Mass: ~4 × 10⁷ M☉
Redshift: z ≈ 10.1
Discovered: 2023 (JWST + Chandra)
Age of universe: ~470 Myr

Discovered by combining JWST infrared and Chandra X-ray data behind galaxy cluster Abell 2744 (gravitational lensing). This is one of the earliest known accreting black holes, existing when the universe was only ~470 million years old. It may represent a "heavy seed" — a direct-collapse black hole from the early universe.

Bogdan et al., Nature Astronomy, 2024
23 billion M☉

APM 08279+5255

Mass: 2.3 × 10¹⁰ M☉
Distance: ~12 Bly (z ≈ 3.9)
Type: Gravitationally lensed quasar
Luminosity: ~10⁴¹ W (lensing magnified)

An extremely luminous quasar whose light is magnified by gravitational lensing from a foreground galaxy. It contains enormous reservoirs of water vapor — equivalent to 140 trillion times all Earth's oceans — making it one of the most water-rich environments in the universe.

Irwin et al., ApJ, 1998; Bradford et al., ApJL, 2011

Intermediate-Mass Black Holes

10² – 10⁵ solar masses. The "missing link" between stellar and supermassive black holes. Their existence was long debated until recent confirmations.

~20,000 M☉

HLX-1 Best IMBH Candidate

Mass: ~2 × 10⁴ M☉
Distance: ~290 Mly
Host: ESO 243-49 (edge-on galaxy)
Type: Hyper-luminous X-ray source

The strongest intermediate-mass black hole candidate. Discovered in 2009, it is located off-center in its host galaxy, suggesting it may be the remnant core of a dwarf galaxy being absorbed. Its X-ray luminosity exceeds 10⁴² erg/s — too bright for a stellar-mass black hole.

Farrell et al., Nature, 2009
~8,200+ M☉

Omega Centauri IMBH 2024

Mass: ≥8,200 M☉
Distance: 17,090 ly
Host: ω Centauri (globular cluster)
Discovery: 2024 (Hubble)

In 2024, Hubble Space Telescope data revealed fast-moving stars in the core of the globular cluster Omega Centauri, confirming the presence of an intermediate-mass black hole. This is significant because Omega Centauri is likely the stripped nucleus of a dwarf galaxy absorbed by the Milky Way billions of years ago.

Häberle et al., Nature, 2024
142 M☉

GW190521 Remnant First Confirmed IMBH

Mass: 142 M☉
Progenitors: 85 + 66 M☉
Distance: ~5.3 Gly
Type: Merger remnant

The merger remnant from the gravitational wave event GW190521 (detected September 2019, announced 2020). This is the first black hole definitively in the intermediate-mass range, formed by the merger of two unusually heavy stellar-mass black holes. The 85 M☉ component falls in the "pair-instability mass gap" (65–120 M☉), where stars are expected to blow themselves apart completely.

LIGO/Virgo Collaboration, PRL, 2020
~50,000 M☉

3XMM J215022.4−055108

Mass: ~5 × 10⁴ M☉
Distance: ~740 Mly
Host: Off-center in a large galaxy
Discovery: 2020 (XMM-Newton + Hubble)

A strong IMBH candidate discovered via an X-ray flare consistent with a tidal disruption event (a star being torn apart by the black hole). The X-ray luminosity and spectral properties indicate a black hole far too massive to be stellar but much less massive than supermassive.

Lin et al., ApJL, 2020
~400 M☉

M82 X-1

Mass: ~400 M☉ (debated)
Distance: ~12 Mly
Host: Messier 82 (starburst galaxy)
Type: Ultra-luminous X-ray source

An ultra-luminous X-ray source (ULX) in the starburst galaxy M82. Quasi-periodic oscillations in its X-ray emission suggest an intermediate-mass black hole, though the mass estimate remains debated. Some models suggest it could be a stellar-mass BH with super-Eddington accretion.

Pasham et al., Nature, 2014

Stellar-Mass Black Holes

~3 – 100 solar masses. Formed from collapsed massive stars or discovered as dormant objects through astrometry. The most common type.

Cygnus X-1 First Confirmed

Mass: 21.2 M☉
Distance: 6,070 ly
Constellation: Cygnus
Companion: HDE 226868 (O9.7 Iab)

The first widely accepted black hole (1971). It accretes matter from its blue supergiant companion star, producing intense X-rays. Subject of a famous bet between Hawking (against) and Thorne (for) — Hawking conceded in 1990. Recent measurements revised its mass upward to 21.2 M☉ and distance to 6,070 ly.

Bolton 1972; Miller-Jones et al., Science, 2021
32.7 M☉

Gaia BH3 Most Massive in MW2024

Mass: 32.7 M☉
Distance: 1,926 ly
Constellation: Aquila
Companion: Metal-poor giant star

Discovered in April 2024 using ESA's Gaia spacecraft data. The most massive stellar-mass black hole found in the Milky Way and the second closest known. It is dormant (no X-ray emission) and was found by tracking the wobble of its companion star. Its companion is very metal-poor, hinting the BH formed from one of the galaxy's earliest massive stars.

Gaia Collaboration, A&A, 2024
9.62 M☉

Gaia BH1 Nearest Known

Mass: 9.62 M☉
Distance: 1,560 ly
Constellation: Ophiuchus
Companion: Sun-like star (G-type)

The closest known black hole to Earth (discovered 2022). Entirely dormant — invisible in X-rays and radio. Found through the gravitational wobble of its Sun-like companion using Gaia astrometry. The orbital period is ~186 days. Its close proximity surprised astronomers.

El-Badry et al., MNRAS, 2023
8.9 M☉

Gaia BH2

Mass: 8.9 M☉
Distance: 3,800 ly
Constellation: Centaurus
Discovery: 2023

The second dormant black hole discovered using Gaia astrometry. It has a remarkably wide orbit (~1,277-day period) with its red giant companion — one of the widest known for a black hole binary. This challenges models of binary stellar evolution.

El-Badry et al., MNRAS, 2023
~9 M☉

V404 Cygni (GS 2023+338)

Mass: ~9 M☉
Distance: ~7,800 ly
Constellation: Cygnus
Companion: K-type star

A low-mass X-ray binary that undergoes dramatic X-ray outbursts. Its 1989 outburst was one of the brightest X-ray events ever recorded. In 2015 it erupted again, producing rapid X-ray variability and jet ejections visible across the electromagnetic spectrum — providing an unprecedented view of black hole accretion physics.

Casares et al., 1992; Motta et al., MNRAS, 2017
6.6 M☉

A0620-00 (V616 Mon)

Mass: ~6.6 M☉
Distance: ~3,500 ly
Constellation: Monoceros
Companion: K-type dwarf

One of the nearest X-ray black holes. It produced the brightest X-ray nova ever observed when it erupted in 1975 (briefly the brightest X-ray source in the sky). Now in quiescence, it is used to study black hole X-ray binary physics in the "off" state.

McClintock & Remillard, 1986
12.4 M☉

GRS 1915+105 First MW Superluminal Jets

Mass: ~12.4 M☉
Distance: ~36,000 ly
Constellation: Aquila
Spin: a > 0.98

The first Milky Way object observed with apparent superluminal (faster-than-light) radio jets — an illusion caused by the jet's near-light-speed motion directed partially toward us. It is one of the most rapidly spinning known black holes and was continuously active in X-rays for over 25 years (1992–2018) before dimming.

Mirabel & Rodríguez, Nature, 1994; Reid et al., 2014
6.3 M☉

GRO J1655-40

Mass: ~6.3 M☉
Distance: ~11,000 ly
Constellation: Scorpius
Type: Microquasar

A "microquasar" — a stellar-mass black hole exhibiting scaled-down versions of the jets and accretion processes seen in quasars. It was the second galactic source observed with superluminal jets. The black hole is thought to have received a natal kick during the supernova, giving the system a high space velocity.

Hjellming & Rupen, 1995; Greene et al., ApJ, 2001
~15.65 M☉

M33 X-7

Mass: 15.65 M☉
Distance: ~3 Mly
Host: Triangulum Galaxy (M33)
Companion: 70 M☉ O-star

The most massive stellar-mass black hole in an eclipsing X-ray binary. Located in the nearby Triangulum Galaxy, it orbits a massive O-type star with a 3.45-day period. The eclipse allows precise mass measurements, making it a benchmark for stellar evolution models.

Orosz et al., Nature, 2007
~10 M☉

VFTS 243

Mass: ≥10 M☉
Distance: 160,000 ly (LMC)
Companion: 25 M☉ O-star
Discovery: 2022

The first dormant stellar-mass black hole discovered outside the Milky Way, found in the Large Magellanic Cloud. Remarkably, the binary orbit is nearly circular, suggesting the star collapsed directly into a black hole without a strong supernova kick — evidence for "direct collapse" black hole formation.

Shenar et al., Nature Astronomy, 2022
~10-20 M☉

SS 433

Mass: ~10–20 M☉ (debated)
Distance: ~18,000 ly
Constellation: Aquila
Type: Microquasar with precessing jets

One of the most exotic stellar systems known. Its twin jets precess like a spinning top with a 162-day period, tracing out corkscrew patterns in space. The jets travel at 26% of the speed of light. The system sits inside the W50 nebula ("Manatee Nebula"), which is being shaped by the jets.

Margon, 1984; Blundell et al., ApJL, 2004
~6.8 M☉

XTE J1118+480

Mass: ~6.8 M☉
Distance: ~5,500 ly
Constellation: Ursa Major
Type: Halo black hole

An unusual "halo" black hole — located far above the galactic plane rather than in the disk where most stars reside. Its high galactic latitude and space velocity suggest it was kicked out of the disk by the supernova that created it, or it formed in a globular cluster.

Wagner et al., 2001; McClintock et al., ApJ, 2001
~7 M☉

LMC X-3

Mass: ~7 M☉
Distance: ~160,000 ly (LMC)
Host: Large Magellanic Cloud
Companion: B-type star

One of the first extragalactic black hole candidates, located in the Large Magellanic Cloud. It is a persistent X-ray source (unlike most transient BH binaries) and has been used extensively to study accretion disk physics and black hole spin measurements.

Cowley et al., 1983; Steiner et al., 2014
~8.5 M☉

MAXI J1820+070

Mass: ~8.5 M☉
Distance: ~10,000 ly
Constellation: Ophiuchus
Outburst: 2018

A transient X-ray binary that underwent a spectacular outburst in March 2018, briefly becoming one of the brightest X-ray sources in the sky. Observations captured relativistic jet ejections in unprecedented detail, with apparent superluminal motion. The jets were also detected in radio, optical, and infrared.

Tucker et al., ApJL, 2018; Bright et al., Nature Astro., 2020

Gravitational Wave Detections

Black hole mergers detected by LIGO, Virgo, and KAGRA through gravitational waves — ripples in spacetime predicted by Einstein in 1916 and first detected in 2015.

36 + 29 → 62 M☉

GW150914 First Ever Detection

Masses: 36 + 29 → 62 M☉
Energy radiated: 3 M☉c²
Distance: 1.3 Bly
Date: Sept 14, 2015

The first direct detection of gravitational waves and the first observation of a binary black hole merger. The signal lasted only 0.2 seconds but released more energy than all stars in the observable universe combined. This earned Kip Thorne, Rainer Weiss, and Barry Barish the 2017 Nobel Prize in Physics.

LIGO/Virgo Collaboration, PRL, 2016
14 + 8 → 21 M☉

GW151226

Masses: 14.2 + 7.5 → 20.8 M☉
Energy radiated: 1 M☉c²
Distance: 1.4 Bly
Date: Dec 26, 2015

The second confirmed gravitational wave detection ("Boxing Day event"). The lower masses meant the signal lasted much longer (~1 second, ~55 orbits in LIGO's band), allowing measurement of the spins — at least one black hole was spinning.

LIGO/Virgo Collaboration, PRL, 2016
31 + 19 → 49 M☉

GW170104

Masses: 31.2 + 19.4 → 48.7 M☉
Energy radiated: 2 M☉c²
Distance: 2.8 Bly
Date: Jan 4, 2017

A merger at cosmological distances. Analysis suggested at least one of the black holes may have had its spin misaligned with the orbital angular momentum — possible evidence that the binary formed through dynamical capture in a dense stellar environment rather than from a binary star system.

LIGO/Virgo Collaboration, PRL, 2017
31 + 25 → 53 M☉

GW170814 First 3-Detector

Masses: 30.5 + 25.3 → 53.2 M☉
Distance: 1.8 Bly
Date: Aug 14, 2017
Detectors: LIGO H + L + Virgo

The first gravitational wave event detected by three observatories (both LIGO detectors plus Virgo in Italy). The addition of Virgo dramatically improved sky localization from ~1,200 deg² to ~60 deg², beginning the era of precision gravitational wave astronomy.

LIGO/Virgo Collaboration, PRL, 2017
85 + 66 → 142 M☉

GW190521 Most Massive Merger

Masses: 85 + 66 → 142 M☉
Energy radiated: 9 M☉c²
Distance: 5.3 Gly
Date: May 21, 2019

The most massive binary black hole merger detected. Both components fall in or near the "pair-instability mass gap" (65–130 M☉), where stellar theory predicts no black holes should form from normal stellar evolution. This suggests they may have formed from previous mergers or in unusual environments. The remnant (142 M☉) is the first confirmed IMBH.

LIGO/Virgo Collaboration, PRL & ApJL, 2020
23 + 2.6 M☉

GW190814 Lightest Secondary

Masses: 23.2 + 2.59 M☉
Mass ratio: ~9:1 (most asymmetric)
Distance: 800 Mly
Date: Aug 14, 2019

Notable for its extreme mass asymmetry and its mysterious secondary component of 2.59 M☉ — too heavy to be a neutron star by most models, too light to be a typical black hole. This falls in the "mass gap" (2.2–5 M☉) between neutron stars and black holes, and its nature remains debated.

LIGO/Virgo Collaboration, ApJL, 2020
NSBH Merger

GW200105 & GW200115 First NS–BH

GW200105: 8.9 + 1.9 M☉
GW200115: 5.7 + 1.5 M☉
Distances: 900 & 300 Mly
Dates: Jan 5 & 15, 2020

The first confirmed detections of neutron star–black hole mergers. These events proved that such mixed binary systems exist and merge within a Hubble time. No electromagnetic counterpart was detected, likely because the neutron stars were swallowed whole without being tidally disrupted.

LIGO/Virgo/KAGRA Collaboration, ApJL, 2021
90+ BBH events

GWTC-3 Catalog (O1–O3)

Total events: ~90 confident detections
BBH mergers: ~80+
NSBH mergers: 2–4
Runs: O1 (2015), O2 (2017), O3 (2019–2020)

The third Gravitational-Wave Transient Catalog contains ~90 confident detections from the first three observing runs of LIGO/Virgo. These events have revealed the mass distribution of merging black holes, provided tests of general relativity, measured the Hubble constant, and constrained models of stellar evolution and binary formation.

LIGO/Virgo/KAGRA Collaboration, PRX, 2023
O4 Run: 2023–2025

O4 Observing Run 2023–2025

Status: O4a + O4b completed
Sensitivity: ~160–190 Mpc (BNS range)
New candidates: Hundreds of alerts
Detectors: LIGO H + L + Virgo + KAGRA

The fourth observing run of LIGO/Virgo/KAGRA ran from May 2023 to June 2025 with significantly improved sensitivity. Hundreds of candidate events were publicly alerted in real-time. Notable results include additional NSBH mergers, high-mass events, and improved constraints on the neutron star equation of state. Full catalog publication is forthcoming.

LIGO/Virgo/KAGRA Collaboration, public alerts (GraceDB)
